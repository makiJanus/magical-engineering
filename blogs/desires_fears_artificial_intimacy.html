Can the Use of Artificial Intelligence and Robotics for Sexual Satisfaction Promote Immoral and Dehumanizing Behaviors?

With the emerging use of artificial intelligence and robotics for sexual satisfaction, new ethical and social concerns arise. These artificial companions could foster dehumanizing and unethical behaviors, and this blog will examine these issues from different perspectives.

One of the main concerns is that an object in the shape of a woman could foster the perception of women as mere objects of sexual satisfaction. This could trigger unhealthy dynamics of violence and submission, similar to those associated with pornography.

Meanwhile, the use of language models raises concerns about the type of emotional relationships that could arise when communicating with these artificial beings. If they can satisfy us comprehensively, adjusting to our needs and desires, what kind of world awaits us in the future?

From the perspective of systems philosophy, humans can be understood as a set of interconnected characteristics that interact coherently with their environment. Simplifying:

  1. Body: Perceives and processes the environment at a pre-linguistic level.
  2. Mind: Gives meaning to experiences through language with judgments and knowledge.

This perspective allows us to see the interaction with artificial beings as a reflection of preconceived ideas about how sexual and emotional interactions should be. According to the theory of the equation of media, people tend to anthropomorphize machines, assigning them social roles based on stereotypes. Thus, the way we interact with artificial companions, whether kindly or cruelly, morally or immorally, depends largely on our own behaviors and desires towards other humans.

The main issue is that artificial companions can now have an appearance very similar to humans. We attribute humanity to what appears to be human. For example, nobody worries about a robotic arm in a factory or a robot serving food at a table, as they do not evoke concerns about human rights. However, if these robots had a human appearance, especially with specific racial characteristics, ethical concerns would arise. Simple sex toys, like dildos, have been marketed without major issues, but expanding them to a full body raises concerns about how humans treat each other, reflected in these machines. It seems to be then an issue of aesthetics and design.p>

To prevent immoral and unhealthy uses that may transfer to human relationships, artificial beings can be designed with mechanisms to prevent objectifying or violent behaviors. For example:p>

  1. Emitting annoying sounds in response to inappropriate behaviors.
  2. Ceasing to function upon detecting inappropriate behavior.
  3. Changing to a color reflecting illness, such as a shade of green, in response to immoral acts.

The aim is for these mechanisms to break the "willing suspension of disbelief," which is the willingness to accept the illusion of interacting with a real being. This way, human-machine interaction can be regulated, addressing the mentioned concerns and creating companions that satisfy us physically and emotionally. Additionally, they can encourage us to become more balanced humans, allowing us to explore a wide range of interactions in a risk-free environment without judgments based on changing cultural norms.

Conclusion

It is inevitable that we perceive interactions with artificial companions as interactions with other humans, depending on their level of anthropomorphism. The misuse of these companions depends on how we desire to interact with them and whether it is possible given their design, reflecting in one way or another how we wish to relate to other humans.

Thus, we can posit that robots and virtual humanoids are not inherently problematic. Through their design, it is possible to regulate human-machine interactions, addressing the aforementioned concerns and creating companions that satisfy us physically and emotionally without resorting to violent, immoral, or unhealthy practices that may transfer to human relationships, while simultaneously providing a safe environment for free exploration.
